The Bhagvad Gita - A brief

The Bhagvad Gita occurs in the Bhishma Parva of the Mahabharata, the world’s longest epic.

It is essentially a dialogue between Sri Krishna (accepted as one of the 10 incarnations of Vishnu) and his friend and disciple for whom he has accepted to be a charioteer during the Great War at Kurukshetra. The book is a collection of 700 verses and touches upon several aspects of spirituality and spiritual practice and duty and how to conduct it apart from other things. The book is accepted as a master piece and along with the Mahabharata has also been celebrated as a book that accurately reflects the human condition in its entirety. It has elements of psychology, profound spiritual philosophy, perspectives for self-conduct and indeed a perspective towards life. The greater Mahabharata also has lessons in governance and other issues.

The Gita is a great global treasure with individuals across national and community boundaries having read it through the centuries and continuing to read it and appreciate its worth. It is for its global appeal that the book belongs to all equally and no community can claim a copy right over the Bhagvad Gita. As readers of The Gita, one of the greatest things we can do is to uphold it as a gift to all humanity from the same Eternal Divinity that we, the people of this world know by different names.

The Bhagvad Gita has been often referred to as the Vedanta-sar or the summary of the Vedanta literature. In a way it is a synopsis of the spiritual ideas of the Upanishads (word meaning: ‘Sitting near the feet’ – of a spiritual master). This is clear in a verse from the meditations that says..

All Upanishads are like cows while Gopal Nandana (Sri Krishna, the Shepherd) is the milkman (who shall milk the cows – Upanishads). Partha is the enjoyer of the milk, while the nectar like milk itself is the great Gita.
